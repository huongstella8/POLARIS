        Adjective Order

        When multiple adjectives precede a noun, they follow this order:

        Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material

        Example: a beautiful tall young oval blue French silk scarf

        In practice 2–3 adjectives are most common: slim elegant elderly woman

        Comparatives & Superlatives

        Short adjectives (1 syllable): add -er / -est — tall → taller → tallest.

        Long adjectives (2+ syllables): use more / most — outgoing → more outgoing.

        Irregular: good → better → best • bad → worse → worst • far → further → furthest

        Use than after comparatives, the before superlatives.

        Module G — Time Expressions & Habits

        G

        Past Simple vs. Used to

        Past Simple — a completed action at a specific time: She visited Paris last year.

        Used to — a past habit or state that no longer exists: I used to play football.

        Both can describe past habits, but used to emphasises contrast with the present.

        Questions: Did you use to…? • Negative: I didn’t use to…

        Module H — Life Stages

        H

        Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

        Present Perfect — connects past to now; used with ever, never, already, yet, just, since, for: I have lived here for three years.

        Past Simple — a finished action at a specific time: I moved here in 2020.

        Key signals: yesterday, last year, ago → Past Simple; recently, so far, up to now → Present Perfect.

        Module I — Relationships, Weddings & Conflict

        I

        Relative Clauses

        Use who for people • which for things • whose for possession • where for places • that for people or things in defining clauses.

        Defining (no commas) — essential: The man who called is my uncle.

        Non-defining (with commas) — extra info, cannot use that: My sister, who lives in London, is a doctor.
